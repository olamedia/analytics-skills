# Trust Signals

The strongest predictors of website trust according to peer-reviewed research. Visual design is only 1 of 10 factors in Stanford's model. The items below have more impact on trust than any typography or color decision.

## Checklist

### Stanford Web Credibility (Fogg 2002, 4,500+ participants)

| Practice |
|---|
| Verifiable accuracy — citations, links to sources |
| Real organization — physical address, office photos |
| Visible expertise — team credentials, affiliations |
| Real people — employee bios with photos |
| Easy contact — phone, address, email prominent |
| Zero errors — typos and broken links destroy credibility |
| Regular updates — visible "last reviewed" dates |
| Restraint with promotion — no excessive pop-ups/ads |

### NNGroup Trust Factors (Nielsen 1999, cross-cultural)

| Practice |
|---|
| Upfront disclosure — prices, fees, policies visible immediately |
| No login walls — provide value before asking for info |
| Real process content — show the work, not just results |
| Content for all customer types — don't gatekeep |
| External reviews — link to third-party review sites |
| Connection to the web — social, press, inbound links |

### Baymard Institute (150,000+ hours user testing)

| Practice |
|---|
| No hidden costs — 48% abandon over unexpected fees |
| No forced account creation — 24% abandon |
| Clear delivery times — 22% abandon over unclear timing |
| Max 5 trust badges — 6+ creates suspicion |
| Security badges near payment form only |
| Specific certifications — generic "Secure Checkout" has no credibility |

## Prototypicality

The single strongest trust predictor (Tuch et al. 2012, 1,530 participants): **look like what users expect a site in your category to look like.**

How to apply:
1. Open 5-10 competitor sites in the same category
2. Note what's consistent: logo placement, nav structure, CTA positioning, color palette range, footer content
3. Match those patterns for your site's skeleton
4. Add brand personality within those conventions — don't break the skeleton

A predictable e-commerce layout (logo top-left, cart top-right, product grid, filters sidebar) IS the trust signal. Novelty in layout creates cognitive friction.

## Real Organization Proof

Users need evidence that a real entity exists behind the website. The more tangible the proof, the stronger the trust.

**What to include:**

| Element | Impact | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Physical address | High | Full street address, not just city. Link to Google Maps. |
| Office/workspace photos | High | Real photos, not stock. Even modest offices are better than no photos. |
| Team page with real bios | High | Name, photo, role, brief background. Personal details (hobbies, pets) humanize. |
| Phone number | High | Visible in header or footer, not buried in a contact form. |
| Email address | Medium | Displayed as text, not just a contact form. Domain email (not gmail). |
| Registration / tax numbers | Medium | Chamber of commerce, company registration, VAT number. |
| Social media links | Medium | Active accounts with real engagement, not empty profiles. |

**What to avoid:**
- Stock photos of "the team" — users spot these and trust drops immediately
- P.O. box only — suggests the organization has something to hide
- Contact form as the only contact method — feels like a wall between user and org

## Pricing and Cost Transparency

Hidden costs are the #1 reason users abandon (48% — Baymard Institute).

**Rules:**
- Show the total price including taxes/fees as early as possible
- If the price varies, show the range or "starting from" price
- List all fees that will be added (shipping, handling, service fees) on the product page, not at checkout
- If pricing requires a quote, explain why and give a typical range
- Never require account creation before showing prices

**Pricing page structure for trust:**
1. Plans/options visible without scrolling
2. Feature comparison table (what's included vs. not)
3. Total price shown — not "from $X" that doubles at checkout
4. Money-back guarantee or free trial terms visible
5. FAQ addressing common pricing questions below

## Trust Badges

Baymard found that trust badges follow an inverted-U curve: some help, too many hurt.

| Count | Effect |
|---|---|
| 0 | No explicit trust signal — neutral |
| 1-3 | Positive — recognizable badges add reassurance |
| 4-5 | Maximum benefit range |
| 6+ | Suspicion increases — "why do they need so many?" |

**Placement rules:**
- Security badges (SSL, PCI, Norton) → near the payment form fields, not in the footer
- Industry certifications → about page or footer
- Customer logos / "as seen in" → social proof section
- Generic "Secure Checkout" text with no backing certification → has no credibility, remove it

**Effective badges:** Norton, McAfee, BBB, industry-specific certifications the audience recognizes.

**Ineffective:** Self-made badges, "100% Secure" stamps, unknown certification logos.

## External Validation

Users trust third-party review sites more than anything on your website (NNGroup).

**What to include:**
- Links to reviews on Google, Trustpilot, G2, Capterra, Yelp — whichever is standard for your industry
- Embedded review widgets with real reviews (not curated best-of)
- Press mentions with logos and links to the articles
- Case studies with named clients (not "a Fortune 500 company")
- Industry awards with verifiable links

**What to avoid:**
- Testimonials with only first names and no photo — looks fabricated
- Star ratings with no review source — unverifiable
- "500+ happy customers" with no way to verify

## Zero Errors — Highest ROI Trust Signal

Stanford found that errors of all types destroy credibility disproportionately. This is the highest-ROI trust investment because it costs nothing but attention.

**What to check:**
- Typos in any visible text
- Broken links (internal and external)
- Missing images / broken image placeholders
- Inconsistent capitalization in nav, headings, buttons
- Placeholder text left in ("Lorem ipsum", "TODO", "Change this")
- Broken interactive elements (buttons that don't work, forms that don't submit)
- Mixed date formats (Jan 5 vs 5/1 vs 2025-01-05)
- Inconsistent terminology (switching between "Sign Up" / "Register" / "Create Account")

A single typo in a headline does more trust damage than a perfect gradient animation does trust good.

## Content Freshness

Users check dates. Old content suggests abandonment.

- Show "Last updated" or "Last reviewed" dates on important pages
- Blog/news section with recent posts (if you have one — an abandoned blog is worse than no blog)
- Copyright year in footer should be current
- If content can't be updated often, use "evergreen" phrasing that doesn't date itself

---

## Common Mistakes

- Investing in visual polish while hiding the phone number — design quality is 1 of 10 Stanford factors; contact info is another
- No external validation — a beautiful site with zero third-party proof is suspicious
- 8 trust badges in the footer — triggers "thou doth protest too much" response
- Stock photos for team page — instantly detected, trust drops below having no team page at all
- Login wall before pricing — 24% immediate abandonment (Baymard)
- Surprise fees at checkout — 48% abandonment, the single biggest trust killer
- Broken links left unfixed — "if they don't care about this, what else don't they care about?"
