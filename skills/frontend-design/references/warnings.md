# Opinionated Claims — WARNING

Popular design advice that is NOT backed by research, or contradicts it. Check against these when reviewing code or evaluating design requests.

---

## 1. "Dark mode is premium"

**Claim:** White/light backgrounds are an anti-pattern; dark mode signals premium quality.

**Research:** No study supports this. NNGroup found color should match service type (green = "fresh" for cleaning companies). Stanford says design must match purpose. Dark mode is a user preference, not a trust signal. A law firm with a dark theme may feel less trustworthy than one with a light theme.

---

## 2. "Ban system fonts for headlines"

**Claim:** Inter, Roboto, Arial, and system fonts are "generic" and should never be used for headlines.

**Research:** Song & Schwarz (2008) — hard-to-read fonts reduce perceived truthfulness. No study shows font *choice* affects trust — only font *readability*. System fonts load instantly (zero FOIT) = better performance = trust signal. Distinctive fonts help brand differentiation but may reduce processing fluency if unfamiliar.

---

## 3. "Pick an extreme aesthetic direction"

**Claim:** Choose one extreme direction — brutally minimal, maximalist chaos, retro-futuristic, etc.

**Research:** Tuch et al. (2012, 1,530 participants) — prototypicality's direct effect on trustworthiness outweighs aesthetics. "Extreme" = low prototypicality = lower trust. This optimizes for memorability, not trust. Use only when the audience expects it (creative agency portfolio, gaming).

---

## 4. "Surprise the user" / "Break expectations"

**Claim:** At least one element should break expectations in a delightful way. Seek unexpected layouts, asymmetry, overlap, diagonal flow, grid-breaking.

**Research:** Processing fluency (Reber & Schwarz 1999, Kahneman System 1): unexpected = harder to process = less trustworthy. Surprise works for entertainment/creative sites where novelty IS the expectation. For business/e-commerce/SaaS, predictability = trust.

---

## 5. "Avoid predictable layouts"

**Claim:** Predictable layouts and component patterns are generic and should be avoided.

**Research:** Directly contradicts prototypicality research. Users trust sites that look like others in the same category. A predictable e-commerce layout (logo top-left, cart top-right, product grid, filters sidebar) IS the trust signal. Layout novelty creates cognitive friction.

---

## 6. Specific font prescriptions

**Claim:** Specific display and body fonts prescribed as universally premium choices.

**Research:** Pure aesthetic opinion. No research links any specific font to trust. What matters: readability, consistent hierarchy, fast loading. Pick fonts matching brand and audience expectations.

---

## 7. Specific color hex prescriptions

**Claim:** Specific hex values (neon orange, neon cyan, etc.) prescribed as accent colors.

**Research:** Pure aesthetic opinion. Color should match service type and industry expectations (NNGroup). A medical site with neon orange may feel less trustworthy than blue/green.

---

## 8. "Neon accents" / "Glow effects"

**Claim:** Neon text-shadow, neon box-shadow, and glow-pulse patterns are core premium visual effects.

**Research:** No research support. Appropriate for tech/gaming/creative contexts only. For most business categories, neon effects reduce prototypicality and increase visual complexity — both anti-trust (Tuch et al. 2012).

---

## 9. "CRT scanlines" / "Film grain" overlays

**Claim:** Scanline and film grain CSS overlays are quality signals.

**Research:** Decorative overlays adding no information, increasing visual noise. Low visual complexity = more trustworthy (Tuch et al. 2012). Use only when retro/cinematic aesthetic is explicitly what the audience expects.
